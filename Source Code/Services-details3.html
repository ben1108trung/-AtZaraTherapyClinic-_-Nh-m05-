Slip, Trip, and Fall: Who can you sue?

If you fall and injure yourself, you may be entitled to sue for compensation for your injuries under Ontario’s Occupier’s Liability Act. Generally, you can sue the owner of the property where you fell, such as a retail store, a school, a hospital, or even a neighbor. Common examples of slip and fall incidents include falling on walkways that have not been cleared of snow or ice, or that have not been repaired, falling on dirty or slippery floors, tripping on objects on the floor, or falling as a result of poor lighting. Limitation periods for slip and fall lawsuits In most circumstances, the law requires that you start your lawsuit within two years of the date of the accident. However, if you are injured on a sidewalk or other area owned by a municipality, you must notify the municipality of your intention to sue within 10 days of the accident, and you must begin your lawsuit within two years of the accident. Other limitations may apply if the property belongs to the Crown. If you miss these deadlines, you may not be able to recover any compensation for your injury or loss.

How do Motor Vehicle Accident Injury Claim works?

​Call your car insurance provider of the accident and any injuries that you have sustained. Regardless of who was at fault for the accident, your insurance provider is responsible for making health and rehabilitation services available to you. If you were a passenger or were outside of a vehicle at the time of your accident, your claim will instead be processed by the driver’s insurance company. Once you claimed your accident, we can take care of the rest. Call us at 416-833-2798 or 416-733-3223 to book an initial assessment. In the assessment session, your Registered Physiotherapist will examine your injuries sustained by accident, discuss with you a treatment plan, begin therapy, and collect all the necessary information to fill out the forms and submit the claim on your behalf.
